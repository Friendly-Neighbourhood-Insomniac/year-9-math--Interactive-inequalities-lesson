1. Introduction to Inequalities

Inequalities are mathematical statements that compare two values or expressions using inequality signs. They show the relationship of one quantity being greater than, less than, or equal to another. Understanding inequalities is essential for solving real-world problems where exact values are not always possible. They help in scenarios such as budgeting, determining limits, and making predictions.

2. Key Term Defined

  • Inequality: A mathematical statement indicating that two expressions are not equal in value. Inequalities use symbols such as \( <, >, \leq, \geq \) to show the relationship.

3. Common Errors and Misconceptions

3.1 Using the Wrong Inequality Signs

Students might confuse inequality symbols, using \( < \) instead of \( \leq \), for example.

Tip: Say the signs out loud when reading them:

  • \( < \): "less than"
  • \( > \): "greater than"
  • \( \leq \): "less than or equal to"
  • \( \geq \): "greater than or equal to"

Practice matching signs to number lines showing inequalities.

3.2 Writing Signs the Wrong Way Around

Writing inequalities like \( 2 < x > 6 \) can cause confusion.

Incorrect Inequality

\( 2 < x > 6 \)

This suggests that \( x \) is greater than 2 and greater than 6, which doesn't make sense.

Correct Inequality: \( 2 < x < 6 \)

This means \( x \) is greater than 2 and less than 6; in other words, \( x \) is between 2 and 6.
